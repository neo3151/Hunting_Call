# Barred / Great Horned Owl Hoot

The hoot of a large owl is the secondary "shock gobble" trigger in a turkey hunter's arsenal, most frequently used at first light before the sun rises.

## Field Application
- **Purpose:** Used to shock a tom turkey into gobbling on his roost at dawn. Turkeys hear the booming, aggressive hoot of a massive predator and involuntarily gobble in response.
- **Barred Owl Cadence:** "Who cooks for you... who cooks for you-allllll."
- **Tools:** Wooden or synthetic resonant chambers that the hunter blows into, producing a deep, hollow tone.

## Engine Analysis & Scoring
Owl hoots are a test of the engine's ability to measure deep, hollow resonance and strict, classic cadences.

### Key Processing Metrics
1. **The 8-Note Rhythm (Barred Owl):** The Rhythm analyzer tracks the exact syllabus structure of the "Who cooks for you" cadence. Standard variations (like the 8-note or the aggressive 9-note laughing sequence) are expected.
2. **Deep Spectral Resonance:** A true owl hoot has almost no high-frequency harmonics. It is a pure, hollow, bass-heavy tone, typically sitting below 500 Hz. The Tone Quality algorithm penalizes raspy, airy overtones resulting from a poorly sealed mouth call.
3. **The Final Slide ("Allll"):** The cadence ends on a sustained, descending note. The Pitch Tracker expects the final second of the sequence to slide smoothly downward to a vibrating stop.

### Scoring Tips
- **Belly Air, Not Cheek Air:** The deep resonance requires pushing a large volume of air from the stomach. "Cheeking" the call will result in a flat, high-pitched whistle.
- **Rhythmic Accuracy:** The cadence is crucial. The engine will ruthlessly penalize sequences that skip the signature rhythmic pauses between the "words" of the call.

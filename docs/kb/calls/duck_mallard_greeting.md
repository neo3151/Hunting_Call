# Mallard Greeting Call

The greeting call is the foundational communication of the hen mallard. It is a rhythmic, confident sequence declaring safety and a welcoming landing zone to circling flocks.

## Field Application
- **Purpose:** Used when ducks are actively looking at the decoy spread. It "greets" them and pulls them down into the final approach.
- **Tools:** Single or double-reed acrylic/polycarbonate duck calls.

## Engine Analysis & Scoring
A mallard greeting call is a highly rhythmic sequence of 5 to 7 distinct quacks that descend slightly in both volume and pitch as the sequence progresses.

### Key Processing Metrics
1. **The Quack Envelope (The "Ear"):** A proper quack is not a simple horn blast. It is composed of a sharp attack (the "kwa"), a burst of harmonic resonance, and a clean closure (the "k"). The OUTCALL engine's Tone Quality metric specifically looks for this structured envelope in every individual note.
2. **Descending Cadence Tracking:** The engine's Rhythm analyzer requires the first quack to be the loudest and longest, with each successive quack becoming slightly shorter and softer. 
3. **Pitch Stability (No Squeaks):** Single reeds are prone to "squeaking" (sudden jumps to a high harmonic) if overblown. The pitch tracking algorithm aggressively penalizes these instantaneous frequency spikes.

### Scoring Tips
- **Air Presentation:** Air must be pushed from the diaphragm using a vocalized syllable like "hut" or "wack" to create the sharp attack and clean closure. Puffy-cheeked blowing will result in a flat, monotone score.
- **The Rhythm:** Do not machine-gun the sequence. Imagine the cadence of the words: "TEN... nine... eight.. seven.. six.. five". The spacing is critical.

# Whitetail Doe Bleat

The doe bleat is the primary vocalization used by female whitetails to communicate location, establish dominance, or signify readiness to breed.

## Field Application
- **Social Bleat:** A long, drawn-out "Maaaa" sound used to casually locate fawns or other does.
- **Estrus Bleat:** A shorter, more urgent and pleading tone. This is the most effective call for drawing a mature buck during the peak rut.
- **Tools:** Most hunters use a "can" call—a small cylinder with a weighted internal reed. Tipping the can over automatically produces a nearly perfect estrus bleat.

## Engine Analysis & Scoring
A doe bleat is characterized by a very steady, high-pitch and a noticeable vibrato (wobble) in older does.

### Key Processing Metrics
1. **The Frequency Ceiling:** The pitch of a doe bleat is significantly higher than a buck grunt, typically sitting between 400 Hz and 600 Hz. The OUTCALL engine tracks this precise window to ensure the caller isn't accidentally mimicking a distressed fawn (which is much higher).
2. **The "Baaa" Envelope:** The sound starts with a sharp attack (the "B") and smoothly transitions into a hollow, sustained "aaa" sound. Tone evaluation looks for this specific two-part envelope structure.
3. **Volume Taper:** The engine expects a louder initial projection that smoothly tapers off over 1-2 seconds. Abrupt stops or stuttering pitches are penalized.

### Scoring Tips
- **Let it Roll:** If using a can call, allow the internal weight to drop smoothly without shaking the can.
- **Pitch Stability (Mouth Callers):** If using a bite-reed to mimic the bleat manually, pitch stability is paramouut. The CREPE algorithm will severely penalize any fluctuation during the sustained note.

# Wild Turkey Yelp & Cluck

The standard yelp is the most common form of communication among wild turkeys. A hen yelps to gather her flock or to indicate her location and readiness to a gobbler. The cluck is a soft, single-note reassurance call.

## Field Application
- **Purpose:** The absolute bread-and-butter of turkey hunting. The "Plain Yelp" (a 3-to-7 note rhythmic sequence) is used to draw a gobbler into shotgun or bow range. 
- **Tools:** The friction call (slate or glass pot and striker), the rubber-and-latex diaphragm mouth call, or the air-operated box call.

## Engine Analysis & Scoring
The turkey yelp is defined by its signature two-note "roll-over."

### Key Processing Metrics
1. **The "Kee-Yauk" Pitch Break:** A proper yelp actually consists of two distinct notes slammed together. It starts with a high, clear "Kee" (often around 1200-1500 Hz) and immediately snaps downward into a raspy, lower "Yauk" (around 400-600 Hz). The OUTCALL CREPE engine meticulously tracks this high-to-low instantaneous pitch break.
2. **The "Front End" vs. The "Back End":** A high-scoring Tone Quality relies on the ratio of clear front-end (the "Kee") to raspy back-end (the "Yauk"). Too much clear whistle sounds domestic; too much rasp sounds unnatural.
3. **The Rhythm Benchmark:** A standard hen yelp sequence is a beautiful, rhythmic "Yauk... Yauk... Yauk... Yauk." The beat-tracking algorithm expects near-perfect metronomic spacing between the notes, but with the notes themselves getting slightly shorter and softer toward the end of the sequence.

### Scoring Tips
- **The "Roll-Over":** When using a diaphragm, the caller must drop their jaw significantly mid-breath to drop the tongue off the reed, creating the snap from high to low. If the pitch slides slowly, the engine will penalize the score.
- **The Reassurance Cluck:** A cluck is simply the "Yauk" half of the yelp, delivered as a single sharp pop. The rhythm analyzer measures it as an isolated, distinct acoustic burst.

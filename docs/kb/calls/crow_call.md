# Crow Locator Call

Crows are intensely loud and universally prevalent in the turkey woods, making their call an ideal "shock gobble" trigger.

## Field Application
- **Purpose:** Used almost exclusively in the spring turkey woods to locate Toms. A sudden, loud crow call causes a Tom to involuntarily "shock gobble" in response, revealing his location without him realizing a hunter is near.
- **Tools:** Wooden or plastic reed-based crow calls, or natural voice calling (cupping the hands over the mouth).

## Engine Analysis & Scoring
The crow call is short, sharp, and highly repetitive, requiring the engine to process staccato bursts.

### Key Processing Metrics
1. **Rhythm & Cadence Analysis:** The most critical factor. Crows almost always call in distinct cadences (e.g., 3-note bursts: Caw-caw-caw). The Rhythm score accounts for 40% of the total rating, measuring the exact temporal spacing between notes.
2. **The "Rasp" Baseline:** A crow call is inherently raspy. The Tone Quality algorithm adjusts its baseline expectations; what would be considered "too much noise" for an elk bugle is required here.
3. **Frequency Peak:** The dominant frequency is sharp and piercing, usually spiking around 1000-1500 Hz. The pitch tolerance band is wider, focusing more on the harshness of the note than the musicality.

### Scoring Tips
- **Bite & Release:** A good crow call is not a long exhale. It requires sharp, forceful bursts of air from the diaphragm. The engine tracks the attack envelope of each note—slow buildups are penalized.
